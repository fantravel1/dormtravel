🎞 As Seen In

The Movies That Made Us Book the Flight

These films didn't just entertain us -- they planted the seed. Every one of them made backpacking Europe look like the adventure of a lifetime. Spoiler: it actually is.

🎞
EuroTrip
2004

The movie that literally named this trip. Scotty and his friends stumble through Europe in the most chaotic way possible -- wrong trains, sketchy hostels, and accidentally ending up in Bratislava. It's ridiculous, it's quotable, and it convinced an entire generation that a Eurail pass was the key to the best summer ever.

🌅
Before Sunrise
1995

Jesse meets Céline on a train and they spend one perfect night wandering Vienna together. No phones, no plans, just conversation and connection. This film is the reason half of all backpackers have a "romantic stranger on a European train" fantasy. Vienna has never looked more magical.

🎶
The Lizzie McGuire Movie
2003

Lizzie's school trip to Rome turned into a pop star adventure at the Colosseum. Sure, you probably won't get mistaken for an Italian celebrity, but the gelato is real, the Trevi Fountain is real, and that feeling of being young and free in Rome? Absolutely real. This movie made every kid want to study abroad.

🌃
Midnight in Paris
2011

Woody Allen made Paris look like a time machine. Walking along the Seine at midnight, stumbling into hidden jazz bars, and feeling like every cobblestone street leads to something extraordinary. After watching this, you'll understand why Paris at 2 AM hits different than anywhere else on earth.

⛵
The Talented Mr. Ripley
1999

Forget the murder plot -- this film is basically a two-hour tourism ad for the Italian coastline. The Amalfi Coast, Rome's piazzas, and the dolce vita lifestyle make you want to drop everything and live off espresso and focaccia in a sun-drenched Italian village. Matt Damon made it look too good.

💌
Letters to Juliet
2010

Set in Verona and the Tuscan countryside, this rom-com turned Juliet's balcony into a bucket list destination. The rolling hills, vineyards, and that golden Italian light make you want to write love letters and eat pasta until you can't move. Peak "I need to go to Italy immediately" energy.

💡 Pro Tip

Watch Before Sunrise on the train to Vienna. Seriously. Download it before you leave and play it on your phone as the Austrian countryside rolls past your window. It hits completely different when you're actually there. Then walk to the same spots Jesse and Céline visited -- most are still exactly the same.

🗺 The Classic Route

The 6 Cities Every EuroTripper Hits

This is the route that millions of college students have taken before you. It's classic for a reason -- each city builds on the last, and by the end you'll feel like a completely different person.

🇬🇧

London

Start here because the language barrier is zero and the Tube will teach you how public transit works in Europe. Hit the free museums (British Museum, Tate Modern, National Gallery -- all free), do a pub crawl through Shoreditch, and get your first hostel experience at Generator London or St Christopher's Inn at London Bridge. The energy of your first morning waking up in a foreign city? Unmatched.

🇫🇷

Paris

Take the Eurostar from London (2.5 hours, book early for $50-$80 fares). Paris is expensive but you can do it cheap: picnic under the Eiffel Tower with baguettes and cheese from a Carrefour, climb Montmartre at sunset for free, and explore the Marais neighborhood. Skip the tourist-trap restaurants near the Louvre and eat where actual Parisians eat. The crêpe stands along the Seine at midnight are a vibe.

🇳🇱

Amsterdam

The most chill city on the route. Rent a bike (this is how locals get around), cruise the canals, visit the Van Gogh Museum and Anne Frank House (book tickets weeks in advance or you won't get in). Stay at The Flying Pig Downtown -- it's legendary among backpackers, with a bar, communal kitchen, and smoking lounge. The Jordaan neighborhood is gorgeous for wandering. Vondelpark on a sunny afternoon is pure happiness.

🇩🇪

Berlin

Berlin is where the trip gets real. It's gritty, creative, and the nightlife is on another level. Visit the Berlin Wall's East Side Gallery, the Holocaust Memorial, and Checkpoint Charlie. Eat döner kebabs at Mustafa's Gemüse Kebap (the line is worth it). The club scene is legendary -- Berghain is the holy grail but incredibly hard to get into. Try Tresor or Watergate instead. Berlin is also dirt cheap compared to London and Paris.

🇨🇿

Prague

Your wallet will thank you. Prague is stunning and absurdly affordable -- a pint of world-class Czech beer costs $1.50, a full dinner is $8, and hostels run $12-$18/night. Walk across Charles Bridge at sunrise before the crowds, explore the castle complex, and get lost in the Old Town's medieval streets. The nightlife in the 5-story clubs is chaotic in the best way. Stay at Mosaic House or Sir Toby's for the best backpacker atmosphere.

🇮🇹

Rome

End with the eternal city because nothing tops throwing a coin in the Trevi Fountain and knowing you'll be back someday. The Colosseum, Vatican, and Pantheon are non-negotiable. Eat pizza al taglio (by the slice) in Trastevere, get gelato from Giolitti or Fatamorgana, and watch the sunset from Pincio Terrace. Generator Rome is perfectly located near Termini station. Rome makes you feel like every day is a scene from a movie.

💡 Pro Tip

Book your Eurail pass as a "flexi" pass -- you pick which days to activate it, so you don't waste travel days when you're staying put in one city. A 10-day-in-2-months flexi pass is perfect for a 3-week trip. And always, always download the Eurail app offline before you board. Train Wi-Fi in Europe is notoriously unreliable.

📅 Day-by-Day Itinerary

21 Days, 8 Cities, One Unforgettable Summer

This is the itinerary we'd give our best friend. It's tested, optimized, and packed with the stuff guidebooks don't tell you. Adjust as needed -- that's the whole point of backpacking.

Days 1-3
🇬🇧 London -- Your Launchpad
You just landed at Heathrow with a backpack and a heart full of chaos. Welcome to Europe. London eases you in gently because everyone speaks English, the Tube is intuitive, and the hostel scene is incredible. Generator London in King's Cross or St Christopher's Village at London Bridge are your best bets -- both have bars, events, and you'll meet your first travel friends within hours. Spend three days soaking in the free museums, wandering through Camden Market, and getting your first proper English breakfast (beans on toast hits different at 7 AM after a night out in Soho).
Morning Day 1: Recover from jet lag with a full English breakfast, then explore the British Museum (free, world-class, plan 3+ hours). Day 2: Tower of London and Tower Bridge walk, then Borough Market for lunch. Day 3: Camden Market and Regent's Park, or hit up Portobello Road in Notting Hill.
Afternoon Day 1: Walk along the South Bank from Westminster Bridge to the Tate Modern (free). Day 2: Covent Garden street performers, then National Gallery at Trafalgar Square (free). Day 3: Shoreditch street art walk and Brick Lane for a cheap curry.
Evening Day 1: Join a free walking tour (tip-based), then pub crawl in Soho. Day 2: Hostel bar social night -- most hostels do organized events. Day 3: Sunset from Primrose Hill (the London skyline view is insane), then Shoreditch nightlife.
Days 4-6
🇫🇷 Paris -- The City That Ruins All Other Cities
Catch the Eurostar from St Pancras to Gare du Nord (book 2+ months early for fares as low as $50). Paris is expensive, but the secret is that the best parts are free: walking along the Seine, climbing Montmartre, picnicking in Luxembourg Gardens, and just sitting at a café watching the world go by. Stay at Generator Paris near Gare du Nord or St Christopher's Canal -- both are social, affordable, and in good neighborhoods. Yes, you need to see the Eiffel Tower. No, you don't need to go up it (the view from Trocadéro is better and free).
Morning Day 4: Arrive from London, check into hostel, grab a croissant and café crème from any boulangerie. Walk to Notre-Dame area. Day 5: Louvre Museum early (go straight to Mona Lisa then explore backward -- way fewer crowds). Day 6: Montmartre at sunrise, Sacré-Cœur steps, artist square at Place du Tertre.
Afternoon Day 4: Eiffel Tower from Trocadéro, then picnic on the Champ de Mars with baguettes, cheese, and cheap wine from Monoprix. Day 5: Musée d'Orsay (the Impressionist collection is jaw-dropping), then wander the Latin Quarter. Day 6: Le Marais neighborhood -- vintage shops, falafel on Rue des Rosiers (L'As du Fallafel is legendary), and Place des Vosges.
Evening Day 4: Seine river walk at golden hour, then drinks at a canal-side bar in Oberkampf. Day 5: Picnic under the Eiffel Tower when it sparkles every hour on the hour (it's real and it's spectacular). Day 6: Hostel bar crawl or the Bastille neighborhood nightlife scene.
Days 7-8
🇳🇱 Amsterdam -- Bikes, Canals, and Good Vibes
Train from Paris to Amsterdam (3.5 hours on the Thalys, use your Eurail pass). Amsterdam is compact, bike-friendly, and impossible not to love. The Flying Pig Downtown is THE backpacker hostel -- the communal vibe is unreal, and it's right in the center. Two days is tight but doable if you prioritize. Rent a bike on day one and just ride -- you'll cover more ground than walking and it's the most Amsterdam thing you can do.
Morning Day 7: Anne Frank House (book online 6 weeks in advance -- they sell out instantly). Then canal walk through the Jordaan neighborhood. Day 8: Van Gogh Museum (student discount available), then Albert Cuyp Market for stroopwafels and herring.
Afternoon Day 7: Rent a bike, ride through Vondelpark, explore the Nine Streets shopping area, grab pancakes at The Pancake Bakery. Day 8: Heineken Experience or A'DAM Tower Lookout for the swing over the edge (the most Instagrammable thing in Amsterdam).
Evening Day 7: Red Light District walking tour (do a legit guided one, not solo wandering), then Leidseplein for bars and live music. Day 8: Brown café pub crawl (these are Amsterdam's cozy, centuries-old pubs), then Rembrandtplein nightlife area.
Days 9-11
🇩🇪 Berlin -- Gritty, Creative, Unforgettable
The overnight train from Amsterdam to Berlin is a rite of passage (6 hours, save a night's hostel cost by sleeping on board). Berlin is the city that will surprise you the most. It's raw, artsy, and the history hits you in the gut. The nightlife is legendary -- clubs don't even open until midnight and go until Monday morning. Stay at Generator Berlin Mitte or Wombat's City Hostel. Döner kebabs are $4 and they're the best drunk food on the continent.
Morning Day 9: Berlin Wall Memorial and East Side Gallery (1.3 km of murals on the remaining Wall -- the most powerful street art you'll ever see). Day 10: Holocaust Memorial (free, deeply moving, go early when it's quiet). Day 11: Flea market at Mauerpark -- live karaoke, vintage finds, and currywurst.
Afternoon Day 9: Checkpoint Charlie and Topography of Terror (free museum on the Gestapo headquarters site). Then Mustafa's Gemüse Kebap -- the line takes 45 minutes but the kebab is a religious experience. Day 10: Museum Island -- pick 1-2 museums (Pergamon or Neues). Day 11: Kreuzberg neighborhood -- street art, Turkish markets, and beer gardens along the Spree River.
Evening Day 9: Pre-game at the hostel, then hit Kreuzberg bars. Day 10: This is your big Berlin night out. Attempt Berghain (dress all black, go alone or in a small group, don't take photos in line). If rejected, Tresor and Watergate are incredible alternatives. Day 11: Chill night at a beer garden in Prenzlauer Berg, or sunset at Tempelhof (an old airport turned into a park).
Days 12-13
🇨🇿 Prague -- Beautiful, Cheap, and Wild
Train from Berlin to Prague (4.5 hours through gorgeous Saxony countryside). Prague is where your budget gets a vacation. Everything is half the price of Western Europe: $1.50 beers, $12 hostel beds, $8 three-course meals. But it's not just cheap -- it's genuinely one of the most beautiful cities in Europe. The Old Town feels like a fairy tale, and the nightlife in the multi-story clubs is chaotic in the best way. Stay at Mosaic House (eco-friendly, great bar, live music) or Sir Toby's (laid-back, communal kitchen, walking distance to everything).
Morning Day 12: Charles Bridge at sunrise (go before 7 AM and you'll have it practically to yourself -- it's pure magic). Then Prague Castle complex -- St. Vitus Cathedral, Golden Lane, and the views over the red rooftops. Day 13: Old Town Square, Astronomical Clock (catch the hourly show), and Jewish Quarter.
Afternoon Day 12: Petrin Hill for panoramic views (take the funicular up, walk down through the gardens). Then Letna Park for a beer with the best view in Prague. Day 13: Explore Vinohrady neighborhood (the local, non-touristy side of Prague), grab trdelník (chimney cake) and visit a traditional Czech beer hall -- try Lok√°l for authentic tank beer.
Evening Day 12: Beer spa experience (yes, you sit in a tub of beer -- it's as amazing as it sounds), then the 5-story Karlovy Lázne club (biggest in Central Europe). Day 13: Pub crawl through the Old Town, then craft cocktails at Hemingway Bar or Anonymous Bar in the underground vaults.
Days 14-16
🇦🇹 Vienna & Budapest -- Imperial Grandeur Meets Ruin Bars
This is the stretch most first-timers skip, and they're wrong. Prague to Vienna is just 4 hours by train, and Vienna to Budapest is another 2.5. Vienna is where Before Sunrise was filmed -- elegant, musical, and surprisingly affordable if you know where to look. Budapest is the wildcard that might become your favorite city of the whole trip. The ruin bars (bars built in abandoned buildings) are unlike anything else in Europe, and the thermal baths are the best way to recover from weeks of backpacking. Stay at Wombat's in both cities -- they're the gold standard for this region.
Morning Day 14: Vienna -- Schoenbrunn Palace gardens (free to walk through), then the Naschmarkt for brunch (best falafel and pastries in the city). Day 15: Train to Budapest, check into Wombat's or Carpe Noctem Vitae (the highest-rated party hostel in Europe). Walk across Chain Bridge. Day 16: Széchenyi Thermal Baths -- bring a swimsuit, soak in the hot pools, play chess in the water like locals.
Afternoon Day 14: Walk the Ringstrasse, visit St. Stephen's Cathedral, then Café Central for Sachertorte and Viennese coffee (it's touristy but genuinely worth it). Day 15: Buda Castle and Fisherman's Bastion for sunset views over the Danube -- this is one of the most photogenic spots in all of Europe. Day 16: Great Market Hall for paprika and Hungarian salami, then the Shoes on the Danube memorial.
Evening Day 14: Find a classical concert at a Viennese church (often free or under $20), then explore the Bermuda Triangle bar district near Schwedenplatz. Day 15: Ruin bar crawl -- start at Szimpla Kert (the original and most famous), then hit Instant-Fogas and Anker't. Day 16: Danube river walk at night when Parliament is lit up, then one more ruin bar for the road.
Days 17-19
🇮🇹 Rome & Florence -- Gelato, History, and La Dolce Vita
Fly budget from Budapest to Rome (Ryanair or Wizz Air, $30-$60 if booked early -- this is the one flight worth taking instead of a 12-hour train). Italy is the grand finale of your Western Europe experience. Rome is overwhelming in the best way -- every corner has 2,000 years of history. Florence is smaller, more walkable, and has the best food in Italy (arguably the world). Stay at Generator Rome or The Yellow (both near Termini). In Florence, try Plus Florence (it has a pool!) or Hostel Gallo d'Oro.
Morning Day 17: Colosseum and Roman Forum (book skip-the-line tickets online, arrive at 8:30 AM opening). Day 18: Vatican Museums and Sistine Chapel (go on a Wednesday for the Papal Audience in St. Peter's Square -- it's free and surreal even if you're not religious). Day 19: Train to Florence (1.5 hours), walk to the Duomo, and just stare at it.
Afternoon Day 17: Trastevere neighborhood for the best pizza al taglio in Rome (try Pizzarium near the Vatican or Supplizio for fried supplì). Throw a coin in the Trevi Fountain. Day 18: Pantheon (free!), Spanish Steps, then gelato crawl -- Fatamorgana for creative flavors, Giolitti for the classic experience. Day 19: Uffizi Gallery (book ahead), then Ponte Vecchio and Piazzale Michelangelo for sunset over Florence.
Evening Day 17: Aperitivo hour in Trastevere -- buy one drink and get a free buffet of Italian appetizers at most bars. Night walk to the Colosseum when it's lit up. Day 18: Campo de' Fiori square for drinks and people-watching. Day 19: Florence aperitivo on the Arno riverbank, then dinner at Trattoria Mario (cash only, communal tables, life-changing pasta for $10).
Days 20-21
🇪🇸 Barcelona -- The Perfect Ending
Fly budget from Florence or Pisa to Barcelona (Vueling or Ryanair, $40-$70). Barcelona is the ultimate last stop because it has everything: beach, architecture, nightlife, tapas, and that Mediterranean energy that makes you never want to go home. Stay at Kabul Party Hostel (right on Plaza Real, legendary atmosphere) or Generator Barcelona (more chill, rooftop terrace). End your trip watching the sunset on Barceloneta Beach with a cold cerveza and the knowledge that you just did something incredible.
Morning Day 20: La Sagrada Familia -- Gaudi's unfinished masterpiece will make your jaw drop (book tickets online, the inside with colored light streaming through the stained glass is unforgettable). Day 21: La Boqueria market on La Rambla for fresh juice and jamón, then wander the Gothic Quarter's medieval alleyways.
Afternoon Day 20: Park Güell (book timed entry), then explore Gràcia neighborhood for local tapas. Day 21: Barceloneta Beach -- swim, sunbathe, play volleyball. Then walk along the waterfront to Port Olímpic. This is your last afternoon in Europe. Soak it in.
Evening Day 20: Tapas crawl through El Born -- patatas bravas, croquetas, pan con tomate, and sangria. Then hit the clubs on the waterfront (Opium, Pacha, or Razzmatazz for live music). Day 21: Final sunset on Barceloneta Beach. Farewell dinner at a chiringuito (beach bar). Raise a glass to three weeks of pure magic. You did it.
💡 Pro Tip

This itinerary is a framework, not a prison. The best EuroTrip moments happen when you ditch the plan: when the Australian you met at the hostel convinces you to take a detour to Cinque Terre, or when you stay an extra night in Budapest because the ruin bars are just too good. Build in 2-3 "flex days" and thank us later.

💡 Pro Tip

🎒 Packing Essentials

3 Weeks in One Backpack

The golden rule of backpacking: if you can't carry it comfortably for 20 minutes through a train station, you packed too much. Here's what actually matters.

🎒

The Right Backpack

Get a 40-50L backpack (Osprey Farpoint 40 is the backpacker gold standard). This size fits as carry-on on most airlines, which saves you $30-$60 per budget flight in checked bag fees. Skip the massive 65L packs -- you'll fill them with stuff you don't need and your shoulders will hate you by day three. A small daypack (packable, 15-20L) for daily exploring is essential too.

👕

Clothing Strategy

Pack for 5 days, do laundry weekly (most hostels have machines for $3-$5). Bring: 5 t-shirts/tops (quick-dry fabrics are a game changer), 2 pairs of shorts, 1 pair of jeans or pants, 1 light jacket, 1 nicer outfit for going out, 7 pairs of underwear, 3 pairs of socks, swimsuit, and a packable rain layer. Roll everything instead of folding -- it saves space and reduces wrinkles. Merino wool base layers don't smell even after multiple wears.

👟

Footwear

Two pairs maximum. One pair of broken-in walking shoes (you'll walk 8-12 miles per day, so comfort is king -- trail runners or lightweight hiking shoes work great) and one pair of sandals or slides for hostels, beaches, and casual evenings. Do NOT bring new shoes. Break them in for at least 2 weeks before the trip or your feet will be destroyed by day two in London.

🔌

Tech & Charging

Universal power adapter (Europe uses Type C/F plugs), portable charger (at least 10,000mAh -- you'll need it on long train days), noise-cancelling earbuds or headphones (essential for sleeping in hostels), and your phone with offline maps downloaded for every city. Bring a small headlamp for navigating hostel dorms without waking everyone up at 3 AM.

🔒

Security Essentials

Money belt or hidden neck pouch for passport and backup cash (keep these on your body in crowded tourist areas). Small combination padlock for hostel lockers (most provide lockers but not locks). Photocopy of your passport stored separately from the original plus a digital copy in your email. A small cable lock for securing your backpack to hostel bed frames overnight gives peace of mind.

🧬

Hostel Survival Kit

Microfiber towel (dries fast, packs small -- many hostels charge $2-$3 for towel rental), earplugs and sleep mask (non-negotiable for shared dorms), flip-flops for shared showers, small toiletry bag with travel-size everything, Ziploc bags for dirty laundry and wet clothes, and a reusable water bottle (tap water is safe to drink throughout Western and Central Europe). Trust us: earplugs will be the best $3 you spend on this entire trip.

💡 Pro Tip

Lay out everything you think you need, then remove a third of it. Seriously. You can buy anything you forgot in Europe (often cheaper than at home), but you can't unbuy the back pain from an overpacked bag. The lightest backpackers are always the happiest backpackers. And bring a few ziplock bags -- they're the most versatile item you can pack (wet swimsuit, snacks, dirty socks, waterproofing your phone).

❓ FAQ

Everything You Need to Know Before You Go

Yes, a Eurail pass is almost always worth it for trips covering 4+ countries over 2-3 weeks. A Global Pass (valid in 33 countries) costs around $400-$600 for youth under 28, which pays for itself after just 3-4 long-distance train journeys. The flexibility to hop on any train without booking individual tickets is invaluable. However, for short trips (1-2 countries), individual tickets booked in advance on Trainline or national rail sites can sometimes be cheaper. Reserve seats early on popular routes like Paris-Amsterdam or anywhere in Italy, as reservation fees ($5-$15) are separate from the pass.

Nearly all major European cities on the classic backpacking route are very safe for college students. Copenhagen, Vienna, Prague, and Amsterdam consistently rank among the safest cities in the world. London, Paris, Berlin, and Barcelona are also safe, though you should stay alert for pickpockets in tourist-heavy areas like the Paris Metro, La Rambla in Barcelona, and the Colosseum area in Rome. General tips: keep your passport and extra cash in a money belt, don't flash expensive electronics, and stick to well-lit streets at night. Solo female travelers report feeling safe across Western and Central Europe.

Both are amazing experiences. Solo backpacking gives you complete freedom to change plans on a whim, forces you out of your comfort zone, and you'll meet incredible people at hostels. Group trips (2-4 friends) are cheaper because you split costs on accommodations and taxis, plus you always have someone to watch your bags. A popular hybrid approach: start with a friend for the first week to build confidence, then split up and go solo for the rest. Hostels with communal kitchens, bar crawls, and free walking tours make it ridiculously easy to meet other travelers regardless of how you start.

The best hostel chains for college backpackers are Generator Hostels (stylish, great bars, locations in London, Paris, Amsterdam, Berlin, Rome, Barcelona), St Christopher's Inns (party-focused with Belushi's bars downstairs, great in London, Paris, Amsterdam, Berlin), and Flying Pig (Amsterdam's legendary backpacker hostel). For budget picks, look at Wombat's (Vienna, Berlin, Budapest), Plus Hostels (Prague, Florence), and Kabul Party Hostel (Barcelona). Book on Hostelworld and sort by "atmosphere" rating. Aim for hostels with 8.0+ ratings, free breakfast, communal kitchens, and organized social events.

Plan for $50-$80 per day in Western Europe (London, Paris, Amsterdam) and $30-$50 per day in Central/Eastern Europe (Prague, Budapest, Berlin). This covers food ($15-$25/day if you mix restaurant meals with grocery store picnics), activities and museums ($10-$20/day, many have free days or student discounts), local transport ($5-$10/day), and drinks/nightlife ($10-$20/day). Budget hack: eat your big meal at lunch when many restaurants offer set menus for half the dinner price, and pre-game at the hostel before going out.

US citizens do not need a visa for tourist stays up to 90 days in the Schengen Area (which covers most of Western and Central Europe including France, Germany, Italy, Spain, Netherlands, Austria, Czech Republic, etc.). The UK allows visa-free stays up to 6 months post-Brexit. Starting in 2025, the EU requires ETIAS authorization (similar to the US ESTA), which costs about 7 euros and is valid for 3 years. Apply online at least 72 hours before departure. Make sure your passport is valid for at least 6 months beyond your travel dates.

The sweet spot is late May to mid-June or September. Summer (June-August) has the best weather and longest days, but it's also the most crowded and expensive. Shoulder season (May, September, early October) offers warm weather, fewer crowds, lower hostel prices, and shorter lines at major attractions. Avoid July-August in popular cities like Barcelona, Rome, and Paris unless you love 95-degree heat and hour-long waits at the Louvre. If you're on a tight budget, September is the gold standard: summer weather, winter prices.

The easiest option is buying a European SIM card when you land. Vodafone, Three, and Orange all offer prepaid SIM cards with 10-30GB of data that work across all EU countries for around $20-$40 for a month. Buy one at any phone shop or even at the airport. Alternatively, get an eSIM through Airalo or Holafly before you leave -- no physical card needed, just activate it on your phone. If you have T-Mobile, your US plan may already include free international data (though speeds can be slow). Always download offline Google Maps for each city before you arrive, and download your Eurail tickets offline too.
